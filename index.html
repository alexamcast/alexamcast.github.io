Prediction and detection of credit card fraud from a dataset of 1000 observations of accounts that have at least one recently declined charge and other demographic and account information. Methods for model selection are Logistic regression, Linear Discriminant Analysis, GAM's, Decisions Trees, and Random Forests and an implementation of a probability threshold of 0.25 to help with selecting the model that overestimates the number of compromised credit cards to help minimize overall costs.

The final chosen predictive model, out of a total of 15 models, was a Random Forest for the following model: fraud ∼ number of declines + age + amount. This model had an AUC (for both training and test sets) of 0.8919.

Prediction of housing price estimates for a collection of ten residential buildings to be sold in January 2020, where the predictions are not to be higher than 10% of the true market prices so the houses sell. The data has 1587 known property transactions that took place in Taipei City and contains information about each property including the transaction price, the district in which the building is located, the land and building areas, the main building material, the numbers of rooms, hallways, and bathrooms, the year and month of the transaction, and the year the building was made.

The above plot shows the residuals vs fitted values. The red line is a scatterplot smoother and represents the average value of the residuals (the difference between the observed and predicted prices) for different fitted values. Since the red line trends above the middle dotted line (which represents zero error, a perfect prediction), this means the true values of the house prices are on average larger than our predicted prices. This plot suggests that our model tends be most accurate for smaller housing prices but tends to underestimate for bigger housing prices

Analysis on an anonymized sleep study with the goal to determine whether the difference in drugs taken can cause a difference in sleep patterns for each patient in the study. After determining the data is not normally distributed, the Wald-Wolfowitz Runs Test and the Mann-Whitney-Wilcoxon Test were ran at a significance level of 5%, and it was concluded that the difference in drugs taken do not cause a difference in sleep patterns for their patients.

RESEARCH

Multi-State Markov Modeling for Student Pathways

(Expected Completion: December 2024)

Diversity in STEM has been difficult to achieve due to the lack of diversity in higher education. A major contributor to the lack of diversity in STEM is the unevenness of student mobility for underserved students in STEM. To get a better understanding of this diversity problem, this paper will track the pathways of students who are enrolled in 4-year universities with the use of multi-state Markov modeling. Prior to this investigation, knowledge of the types of enrollment mobility patterns is crucial to properly classify the states of the Markov model through a continuous timeframe. To approach student mobility with multi-state Markov modeling, states such as Math, STEM (Non-Math), and Non-STEM majors were created as well as Graduated and Leave states. These states will help with the investigation of the retention and attrition of STEM majors at both a department level (mathematics) and university level. Furthermore, variables chosen for this modeling method will be selected to consider underserved students so that we can detect whether a relationship between an underserved demographic and student mobility pathways exist.

The dataset used to evaluate student mobility pathways is the MIDFIELD dataset. This data set comes from a longitudinal study from a collection of universities that are in partnership with MIDFIELD. MIDFIELD contains records of student demographic information, academic records such as grades, GPA, and courses, and records of degree attainment information for each student who had graduated from institutions that participated in the study. With the use of multi-state Markov modeling (MSM), we can describe how students move through a series of states during their enrollment period and find the probability of them taking part in different student mobility patterns based on the observed data from MIDFIELD.
